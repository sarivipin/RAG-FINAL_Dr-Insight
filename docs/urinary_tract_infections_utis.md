# Urinary tract infections (UTIs)

## Question
What are the symptoms of urinary tract infections (utis)?

### Answer
Symptoms of a UTI include:

- needing to pee suddenly or more often than usual
- pain or a burning sensation when peeing
- smelly or cloudy pee
- blood in your pee
- pain in your lower tummy
- feeling tired and unwell
- changes in behaviour in older people - such as severe confusion or agitation

UTI symptoms may be difficult to spot in people with dementia.

Pain when you pee can also be a symptom of a sexually transmitted infection (STI).

Symptoms of a UTI in children
Children with UTIs may also:
- appear generally unwell
- wet the bed or wet themselves
- hold in their pee because it stings
Babies may be irritable, not feed properly and have a high temperature (fever) .

## Question
What causes urinary tract infections (utis)?

### Answer
UTIs are usually caused by bacteria from poo entering the urinary tract. The bacteria enter through the tube that carries pee out of the body (urethra).

Women have a shorter urethra than men. This means bacteria are more likely to reach the bladder or kidneys and cause an infection.

Causes of UTIs include:

- pregnancy
- conditions that block the urinary tract - such as kidney stones
- conditions that make it difficult to fully empty the bladder - for example, an enlarged prostate gland in men or constipation in children
- urinary catheters - a tube in your bladder used to drain urine)
- having a weakened immune system - for example, from chemotherapy, HIV or type 2 diabetes

## Question
What should I know about urinary tract infections (utis) and diagnosing a uti?

### Answer
Your GP will ask you about your symptoms.

You may need to give a sample of your pee to test. The test helps to rule out other conditions that might cause your symptoms.

Men are sometimes offered a painless swab test to check for other conditions. This is where a cotton bud is wiped on the tip of the penis and sent for testing. It does not hurt but it may feel uncomfortable.

## Question
What should I know about urinary tract infections (utis) and treating utis?

### Answer
Your GP may prescribe antibiotics to treat a UTI.

Symptoms usually start to clear up within 5 days in adults and 2 days in children. But it's important to finish the whole course of antibiotics, even if you start to feel better.

If you have a severe UTI, your GP may refer you to hospital for treatment and tests. You may need to stay for a few days. Hospital treatment is more likely for men and children with a UTI.

Treating recurring UTIs
If your UTI comes back any time after treatment, your GP may prescribe a longer course of antibiotics.
If you keep getting UTIs and regularly need treatment, your GP may give you a repeat prescription for antibiotics.

Things you can do yourself
Mild UTIs often pass within a few days.
To help ease pain while your symptoms clear up:
- take paracetamol - you can give children liquid paracetamol
- place a hot water bottle on your tummy, back or between your thighs
- rest and drink plenty of fluids - this helps your body to flush out the bacteria
It may also help to avoid having sex until you feel better. You cannot pass a UTI on to your partner but sex may be uncomfortable.
If you have a kidney infection, do not take non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) without talking to your GP. For example, ibuprofen or aspirin.
