# Ectopic pregnancy

## Question
What should I know about ectopic pregnancy and ectopic pregnancy causes?

### Answer
Most of the time it's impossible to say why an ectopic pregnancy happened.

Things that can increase your chance of an ectopic pregnancy include:

- previous ectopic pregnancy
- previous surgery on the fallopian tubes
- damage to the tubes by infections like Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
- smoking
- fertility treatments like in vitro fertilisation (IVF) or intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI)
- becoming pregnant while using certain types of contraceptive such as the coil
- age - if you are over age 40 when you become pregnant

## Question
What should I know about ectopic pregnancy and how an ectopic pregnancy develops?

### Answer
When the ectopic pregnancy implants in your fallopian tube it will grow. You might notice pain early on in the pregnancy. Sometimes there will be bleeding from your vagina. This is dangerous.

Your fallopian tubes are narrow. As the pregnancy develops the tube can stretch. In some cases, the tube can burst. This can cause severe pain in your tummy and internal bleeding.

A pregnancy cannot survive in the fallopian tube.

## Question
What should I know about ectopic pregnancy and why an ectopic pregnancy happens?

### Answer
Before you become pregnant, the ovary releases an egg. This egg travels down your fallopian tube towards the womb. It meets the sperm in the fallopian tube. This is where fertilisation happens.

The fertilised egg then moves down the fallopian tube to the womb.

In a regular pregnancy the egg will implant in the womb, where the baby will grow.

In an ectopic pregnancy the fertilised egg implants outside the womb. This is usually in the fallopian tube.

The egg can implant in other locations outside the womb such as:

- your cervix (the neck of your womb)
- your ovary (rare)
- inside your abdomen (very rare)

More than 1 baby
It is possible, but very rare, for more than 1 baby (for example, twins) to develop outside the womb at the same time.
In extremely rare cases (about 1 in 30,000), an ectopic pregnancy and a normal pregnancy can develop at the same time. This is called heterotopic pregnancy.
